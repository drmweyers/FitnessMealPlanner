Gluten-Free Basics

Gluten is a family of proteins found in wheat, barley, and rye. It gives bread its chewy texture and helps dough rise. For some people, eating gluten triggers serious health issues — for others, avoiding it is a lifestyle preference. Here's what you need to know.

? What Exactly Is Gluten?

Gluten is composed of two proteins — glutenin and gliadin — that form an elastic network when flour is mixed with water. It's found in wheat (including spelt, kamut, farro, durum), barley, rye, and triticale. Oats are naturally gluten-free but are often cross-contaminated during processing.

⚖ Three Reasons People Go GF
Celiac Disease

Autoimmune disorder where gluten damages the small intestine lining. Affects ~1% of the population. Even trace amounts cause harm. Requires strict lifelong avoidance.

Gluten Sensitivity

Non-celiac gluten sensitivity (NCGS) causes bloating, fatigue, and brain fog without intestinal damage. Estimated 6–7% of adults. Symptom-managed avoidance.

Lifestyle Choice

Some people report better energy and digestion without gluten. No medical requirement, but still benefits from knowing safe foods. Flexible approach.

! Hidden Sources of Gluten
These Foods Secretly Contain Gluten
These are the sneaky ones that catch people off guard — always check labels.
  • Soy sauce — brewed with wheat (use tamari instead)
  • Beer & malt beverages — barley-based
  • Salad dressings — wheat-based thickeners
  • Marinades & sauces — flour as thickener
  • Imitation crab — wheat starch binder
  • Seasoning blends — anti-caking agents with wheat
  • Soup & broth — flour-thickened bases
  • Candy & licorice — wheat flour coating
  • Processed meats — fillers & binders
  • Communion wafers — wheat-based

Safe Grains & Starches

Going gluten-free doesn't mean giving up grains entirely. These naturally gluten-free options are nutritious, versatile, and widely available. Here's what to stock in your pantry — with protein and fiber per cooked cup.

Tapioca
0.3g protein • 0g fiber
Great thickener & binder
Also Safe
  • Arrowroot — starch thickener (0.1g P, 0g F)
  • Sorghum — ancient grain (10g P, 6g F)
  • Amaranth — high protein seed (9g P, 5g F)
  • Teff — Ethiopian grain (10g P, 7g F)
  • Sweet potato — nutrient-dense (4g P, 6.5g F)
A Note on Oats
Oats are naturally gluten-free, but conventional oats are almost always cross-contaminated with wheat during growing and processing. Always buy oats labeled "Certified Gluten-Free" from brands like Bob's Red Mill GF, GF Harvest, or Avena Pure Oats.

Flour Substitution Guide

One of the trickiest parts of going gluten-free is replacing wheat flour. Every alternative behaves differently — some absorb more liquid, some don't bind well. This chart gives you exact ratios and best-use scenarios.

GF Flour Ratio to Wheat Best For Notes
Almond Flour 1:1 Cookies, cakes, pancakes Dense, moist. Add extra egg for structure.
Coconut Flour 1/4 : 1 Muffins, quick breads Very absorbent. Triple the liquid. Use extra eggs.
Rice Flour 7/8 : 1 Breading, thickening sauces Gritty alone. Blend with tapioca starch.
Oat Flour (GF) 1:1 Cookies, pancakes, crumbles Make your own: blend GF oats. Mild flavor.
Cassava Flour 1:1 Tortillas, flatbreads, pizza Closest wheat texture. AIP-friendly. Nut-free.
Tapioca Starch 2 Tbsp : 1 Tbsp Thickening, chewy texture Use as partial blend. Creates stretch & chew.
Buckwheat Flour 1:1 Pancakes, crepes, noodles Strong earthy flavor. Mix 50/50 with rice flour.
Arrowroot Starch 1 tsp : 1 Tbsp Thickening sauces, gravies Better than cornstarch for acidic sauces.
Pro Tip: The GF Blend Formula
For an all-purpose GF flour blend that works in most recipes, mix:
2 cups rice flour + 2/3 cup potato starch + 1/3 cup tapioca starch.
Add 1 tsp xanthan gum per cup of blend for binding.
Binding Without Gluten
Gluten provides structure. Without it, add binders:
• Xanthan gum — 1 tsp per cup of GF flour
• Psyllium husk — 1 Tbsp per cup (bread)
• Flax egg — 1 Tbsp ground flax + 3 Tbsp water
• Extra eggs — 1 extra egg per recipe

Reading Labels Like a Pro

Knowing safe ingredients is only half the battle. You need to decode packaging, understand certifications, and navigate restaurants safely. Here's your cheat sheet.

✓ Certification Symbols to Trust
GF
GFCO Certified — Gluten-Free Certification Organization. Products test below 10 ppm gluten. The gold standard. Look for the circular "GF" logo.
CSA
CSA Recognition Seal — Celiac Support Association. Annual testing, strict facility audits. Trusted by celiac community.
NSF
NSF Gluten-Free — NSF International certification. Independent lab testing at <20 ppm. Common on supplements.
FDA
"Gluten-Free" Label (FDA) — Must contain <20 ppm gluten. Not third-party verified — relies on manufacturer testing. Acceptable but less rigorous.
! Red Flags on Ingredient Lists
Avoid These Words
  • Wheat, barley, rye, triticale
  • Malt, malt extract, malt syrup
  • Brewer's yeast
  • Modified food starch (if from wheat)
  • Hydrolyzed wheat protein
  • Seitan, fu (wheat gluten)
  • Semolina, durum, einkorn, spelt
Cross-Contamination Warnings
  • "Made in a facility that also processes wheat"
  • "May contain wheat/gluten"
  • "Processed on shared equipment"

For celiac: avoid these products. The warnings are voluntary — companies who include them are being honest, but the risk is real.

For sensitivity: usually safe, but note how you feel.

★ Restaurant Survival Tips
Do
  • Call ahead and ask about GF prep areas
  • Request grilled proteins with no marinade
  • Ask for rice, potato, or corn-based sides
  • Use apps: Find Me GF, Nima sensor
  • Tip well — servers who help are gold
Don't
  • Assume "wheat-free" means GF
  • Trust fried foods (shared fryer oil)
  • Forget sauces & dressings (ask for plain)
  • Feel embarrassed asking questions
  • Skip the allergy card (carry one!)
