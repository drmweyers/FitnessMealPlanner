Chapter 01

How Fasting Works

Understanding the metabolic cascade that happens when you stop eating — and why the timing matters as much as the food itself.

Insulin Sensitivity

When you fast, insulin levels drop significantly. Low insulin signals your body to switch from glucose-burning to fat-burning mode. Over time, regular fasting improves your cells' response to insulin, making nutrient partitioning more efficient and reducing fat storage from meals.

Autophagy

Your body's cellular recycling program. During extended fasting (16+ hours), cells begin breaking down and recycling damaged proteins and organelles. This process clears out dysfunctional mitochondria, misfolded proteins, and cellular debris — essentially taking out the biological trash.

Growth Hormone Spike

Fasting can increase human growth hormone (HGH) secretion by 300–500%. HGH preserves lean muscle tissue during calorie restriction, accelerates fat metabolism, and supports tissue repair. This is why fasting paired with resistance training preserves muscle.

Fat Oxidation

After glycogen stores deplete (12–16 hours), your body upregulates lipolysis — the breakdown of stored triglycerides into free fatty acids. These fatty acids are then oxidized in the mitochondria for energy. You become a more efficient fat-burning machine.

Fasting Timeline: What Happens at Each Stage

0 – 12 Hours

Fed State → Post-Absorptive State

Blood sugar and insulin normalize. Liver glycogen begins depleting. Your body still primarily uses glucose for fuel. By hour 12, glycogen stores are roughly 50% depleted and the metabolic switch to fat begins.

12 – 16 Hours

Fat-Burning Zone Begins

Insulin drops to baseline. Lipolysis accelerates. Ketone body production starts in the liver. Norepinephrine rises, increasing alertness and metabolic rate by 3.6–14%. Mild autophagy begins in most tissues.

16 – 24 Hours

Deep Ketosis & Autophagy

Significant autophagy activation. Growth hormone surges (up to 5x baseline). Ketone levels reach 0.5–2.0 mmol/L. Brain begins preferring ketones for fuel. Anti-inflammatory gene expression increases. Gut lining begins repair cycle.

24 – 36 Hours

Maximum Autophagy

Peak cellular cleanup. AMPK (cellular energy sensor) is fully activated. Old immune cells are cleared for recycling. Stem cell regeneration signals begin. Insulin sensitivity reaches optimal levels. Not recommended for beginners.

36 – 48 Hours

Extended Repair & Reset

Immune system regeneration accelerates. Significant reduction in inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6). Neuroplasticity and BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) increase. Only for experienced fasters under medical guidance. Electrolyte management becomes critical.

Key Insight: The Best Protocol Is the One You Can Sustain

Research consistently shows that adherence matters more than the specific protocol. A 16:8 fast maintained for 12 months will always outperform a perfect OMAD routine abandoned after 3 weeks. Start easier than you think you need to, then progress once habits are locked in.

Chapter 04

Meal Timing & Training

How to optimize your workouts within a compressed eating window — and whether fasted training is right for you.

Fasted Training

  • Best for: steady-state cardio, light-to-moderate resistance training, fat-loss focus
  • Train in final 2–4 hours of fast (e.g., 10am–12pm on a 16:8)
  • Higher fat oxidation rate: 20–30% more fat burned vs fed cardio
  • May reduce peak power output by 5–10% on heavy compound lifts
  • Follow with your first meal within 60 minutes post-workout
  • Hydrate with water, black coffee, or electrolytes during session

Fed Training

  • Best for: heavy strength training, HIIT, muscle hypertrophy goals
  • Eat a meal 2–3 hours before training for optimal performance
  • Higher glycogen = better peak power, more reps, heavier loads
  • Pre-workout meal: 30–40g protein + 40–60g carbs + low fat
  • Post-workout: 30–40g protein + carbs within 2 hours
  • Schedule training early in your eating window for two post-workout meals

The BCAA Debate: During Fasting

BCAAs (branched-chain amino acids) technically break a fast because they trigger an insulin response and stimulate mTOR, which halts autophagy. However, if your goal is muscle preservation during fasted training (not autophagy), 5–10g BCAAs before a fasted workout may help reduce muscle breakdown. Our recommendation: Skip BCAAs if autophagy/longevity is the goal. Use them if you're lean-bulking or in a deep cut and training hard while fasted. Better yet — schedule your training at the end of your fast so your post-workout meal handles recovery.

Supplements That Don't Break a Fast

Black coffee, plain tea, water, electrolytes (no sugar), creatine monohydrate, vitamin D, fish oil (minimal calories), apple cider vinegar

Supplements That DO Break a Fast

BCAAs, protein powder, pre-workouts with sugar/calories, gummy vitamins, collagen peptides, any supplement with >1g protein or sugar

Chapter 06

Breaking Your Fast

What you eat first matters. The wrong foods can spike insulin, cause bloating, and undo the benefits of your fast.

The Ideal Fast-Breaking Sequence

1

Start with Water + Electrolytes (10 min before eating)

Drink 500ml of water with a pinch of sea salt and a squeeze of lemon. This rehydrates your digestive system, primes stomach acid production, and prevents the bloating that comes from eating on a completely empty, dehydrated gut.

2

Protein + Fiber First (your first bite)

Lead with 30–40g of protein (eggs, chicken, fish, Greek yogurt) paired with fiber-rich vegetables or greens. Protein blunts the glucose spike from carbs eaten later. Fiber slows gastric emptying and feeds gut bacteria that were resting during the fast. Never break a fast with pure sugar or refined carbs.

3

Add Healthy Fats (with your meal)

Include a moderate fat source: avocado, olive oil, nuts, or fatty fish. Fats slow digestion further, improve satiety, and help absorb fat-soluble vitamins (A, D, E, K) that you may be supplementing. Avoid fried or heavily processed fats.

4

Complex Carbs Last (15–20 min in)

Save starches and carbs for after protein and fats are in your system. This "food sequencing" can reduce post-meal glucose spikes by up to 40% (research from Weill Cornell Medicine). Rice, potatoes, bread, fruit — all fine, just not first.

Daily Electrolyte Protocol During Fasting

  • Sodium: 2,000–3,000mg/day — sea salt in water, bone broth, or electrolyte tablets
  • Potassium: 1,000–2,000mg/day — lite salt (potassium chloride), avocado, spinach in meals
  • Magnesium: 300–400mg/day — magnesium glycinate before bed (aids sleep + prevents cramps)
  • Hydration: Minimum 2.5–3.5L water daily, more if training or in hot climates
