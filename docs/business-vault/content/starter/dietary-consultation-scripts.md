# Dietary Consultation Scripts

## How to Discuss Nutrition Without Crossing Scope — From Allergies to Emotional Eating

---

### How to Use These Scripts

These are real conversation frameworks for the five nutrition discussions you'll have most often with clients. Each script includes the context (when you'll need it), the conversation itself (what to say), and notes (what to watch for and where the boundaries are).

Don't memorize these word-for-word. Read them, understand the structure, and then practice delivering the key points in your own voice. The goal is to sound like you — not like you're reading from a playbook.

Every script includes scope-of-practice guidance. Personal trainers can provide general nutrition education and customized meal planning. They cannot diagnose medical conditions, prescribe therapeutic diets, or provide clinical nutrition therapy. These scripts are designed to keep you firmly on the right side of that line.

## Script 1: Explaining Macros to Beginners

### Context

A new client has never heard of macros — or has heard the word but doesn't know what it means. They might be confused by the numbers on their meal plan. They might have seen "IIFYM" on Instagram and want to know what it's about. Or they simply asked, "What are macros and why do they matter?"

This is one of the most common conversations you'll have. Get it right and the client understands their plan at a deeper level, follows it more consistently, and asks smarter questions going forward.

### The Conversation

**Open with a simple frame:**

> "Great question. Macros is short for macronutrients. There are three of them, and everything you eat is made up of some combination of the three. Think of them like building materials for your body."

**Explain each one simply:**

> "The first is protein. Protein builds and repairs your muscles, supports your immune system, and keeps you feeling full. When you're training, protein is the most important macro to get right. Good sources are things like chicken, fish, eggs, Greek yoghurt, and lean beef."

> "The second is carbohydrates. Carbs are your body's primary energy source — they're what fuels your workouts and your brain. Carbs are in things like rice, potatoes, oats, fruit, bread, and pasta. They're not the enemy — they're your fuel."

> "The third is fats. Healthy fats support your hormones, your brain function, and your joints. They're in things like nuts, avocados, olive oil, and salmon. You need fats — the key is choosing quality sources and not overdoing the quantity."

**Connect it to their plan:**

> "On your meal plan, you'll see each meal has a macro breakdown — grams of protein, carbs, and fats. I've calculated these specifically for your goals. Your protein is set at [X]g per day because [reason — e.g., you're in a muscle-building phase]. Your carbs are at [X]g because [reason — e.g., you're training 4 days a week and need the fuel]. And your fats are at [X]g to keep your hormones and recovery on track."

**Make it practical:**

> "You don't need to obsess over hitting exact numbers. Think of it as a target zone — within 10-15 grams either way on each macro is perfectly fine. The goal is consistency over time, not perfection on any given day."

**Handle the inevitable follow-up — "Do I need to track everything?"**

> "For the first two weeks, I'd recommend tracking loosely — even just eyeballing portions based on the plan. After two weeks, most people develop a feel for what a day of eating looks like at their targets. If you're someone who likes data, a food tracking app can help. But it's not required — your meal plan already has the portions dialled in. Follow the plan and the macros take care of themselves."

### Notes

- Avoid getting technical. Clients don't need to know about gluconeogenesis, insulin response, or amino acid profiles. They need to know what to eat and roughly how much.
- If the client starts asking about specific macro ratios (e.g., "Should I be 40/40/20?"), redirect to their plan: "Your plan is already set up with the right ratios for your goals. Let's follow the plan for a month and see how your body responds before we start adjusting ratios."
- Never tell a client that a specific macro ratio will cure or treat a medical condition. That's clinical territory.

## Script 2: Handling "I Hate Meal Prep"

### Context

Your client received their meal plan, looked at the recipes, and immediately felt overwhelmed. "I hate cooking." "I don't have time for meal prep." "This looks like a lot of work." This is one of the most common barriers to adherence, and your response determines whether the client follows the plan or abandons it.

The key insight: the client isn't actually saying they hate cooking. They're saying the plan feels like too much work for their current life. Your job is to reduce the perceived effort, not lecture them on the importance of preparation.

### The Conversation

**Validate first. Don't dismiss.**

> "I hear you, and you're not alone — most of my clients feel that way at first. The good news is you don't need to be a chef or spend your whole Sunday prepping food. Let me show you what this actually looks like in practice."

**Reframe meal prep:**

> "When people hear 'meal prep,' they picture spending 4 hours in the kitchen making 21 meals. That's not what I'm asking you to do. Here's what I mean by prep: spend 60-90 minutes once a week — that's it. In that time, you're going to cook two protein sources, make one batch of carbs, and chop your vegetables. That gives you 80% of what you need for the week."

**Give them the minimal viable prep:**

> "Here's the simplest version: on Sunday, cook a batch of chicken and a batch of rice. That covers lunches for the week. For dinners, your plan includes 3-4 recipes that take 15-20 minutes to make fresh. Breakfasts are things like overnight oats or eggs — five minutes max. That's it. You're spending about an hour on Sunday and 15-20 minutes on dinner each night."

**Offer alternatives if they still resist:**

> "If even that feels like too much right now, let's start smaller. Follow the plan for just 3 days this week. Prep those 3 days only. See how it goes. Once you see how simple the recipes actually are, most people realise it's way less work than they imagined. And if there are specific recipes that feel complicated, tell me and I'll swap them for simpler ones."

**Address the time objection directly:**

> "Can I ask — how much time are you currently spending on food each day? Between deciding what to eat, ordering takeout, waiting for delivery, or standing in front of the fridge? Most people spend more time being indecisive about food than they would following a plan. The plan actually saves time because the decisions are already made."

**If they truly won't prep, offer a no-prep plan:**

> "If cooking really isn't going to happen for you right now, I can rebuild your plan around no-cook or minimal-cook options. Things like pre-made rotisserie chicken, pre-washed salad kits, canned tuna, overnight oats, protein shakes, deli meats, and pre-cut vegetables. It won't have the same variety, but it'll hit your macros and you won't have to turn on a stove. Want me to do that?"

### Notes

- Never guilt a client for not wanting to meal prep. Statements like "Well, you have to prep if you want results" are technically true and practically useless. Meet them where they are.
- EvoFit Meals allows you to filter for prep time and cooking complexity. Use this to build plans with shorter prep times for resistant clients.
- The "just 3 days" approach works remarkably well. Small wins build momentum.
- Some clients will genuinely never meal prep. Build them a plan that works anyway. An imperfect plan they follow beats a perfect plan they ignore.

## Script 3: Scope Boundaries — What You Can and Can't Advise On

### Context

A client asks you something that falls outside your scope as a personal trainer. Maybe they want specific advice about managing their diabetes through diet, or they're asking about an eating disorder, or they want you to interpret blood work results. This happens to every trainer, and how you handle it defines your professionalism.

The goal is to be honest about your boundaries without making the client feel rejected or dismissed. You want them to get the right help while maintaining their trust in you.

### The Conversation

**When a client asks about managing a medical condition through diet:**

> "That's a really important question and I'm glad you brought it up. Here's where I want to be straight with you: as a personal trainer, I can build you meal plans for general health and fitness goals. But when it comes to managing [their condition] through diet, that's something that really needs a registered dietitian or your doctor to weigh in on. They can prescribe a dietary protocol specifically for [condition], and I can build your meal plan around their recommendations."

> "What I'd love to do is this: get a clear directive from your healthcare provider about what your dietary parameters should be — what to include, what to avoid, any specific nutrient targets. Once I have that, I can incorporate those guidelines into your meal plan so everything works together. That way you get the medical expertise where you need it, and the practical meal planning where I can help. Does that work?"

**When a client brings up disordered eating patterns:**

> "Thank you for trusting me with that. I want to make sure you get the right kind of support here. What you're describing sounds like something that would really benefit from working with a professional who specialises in eating behaviours — a therapist or counselor who focuses on this area. I'm not qualified to guide you through that, and I'd be doing you a disservice if I tried."

> "What I can do is continue to provide you with a balanced, non-restrictive meal plan that supports your overall health. And if you start working with a specialist, I'll happily coordinate your meal plan with whatever approach they recommend. Would you like me to look into some referral options for you?"

**When a client asks about interpreting blood work:**

> "I can see why you'd want to understand what those numbers mean for your nutrition. The honest answer is that interpreting blood work is outside my lane — that's your doctor's job. What I'd suggest is: take your results to your next appointment and ask your doctor specifically if there are any dietary changes they recommend based on what they see. Then bring those recommendations back to me and I'll adjust your plan accordingly."

**When a client asks about supplements for a medical purpose:**

> "I can share general information about common supplements that support fitness goals — things like protein powder, creatine, or fish oil. But when it comes to using supplements to treat or manage [condition], that's medical territory and I'd want your doctor or a registered dietitian to advise on that. Supplements can interact with medications and medical conditions in ways that aren't always obvious, so it's worth getting professional guidance."

**The universal structure for scope questions:**

1. Thank them for asking
2. Be clear about what's outside your scope (and why)
3. Redirect to the right professional
4. Offer what you CAN do to support them within your scope
5. Offer to coordinate with the referred professional

### Notes

- Never try to "help a little" with something outside your scope. Partial advice on medical nutrition can be harmful and exposes you to liability.
- Keep a referral list of local registered dietitians, therapists who specialise in eating disorders, and sports medicine doctors. Having names ready makes the referral feel helpful, not dismissive.
- If a client discloses an active eating disorder, take it seriously. Don't continue providing meal plans that could be misused in the context of an eating disorder without guidance from a qualified professional.
- Document scope referrals in your client notes. If a client discloses a medical condition and you referred them to a specialist, note the date, the disclosure, and the referral. This protects both of you.
- Scope awareness is not weakness — it's professionalism. Clients respect trainers who know their limits more than trainers who pretend to know everything.

## Script 4: Handling Supplement Questions

### Context

Every client will eventually ask about supplements. "Should I take protein powder?" "What about creatine?" "My friend takes fat burners, should I?" "I saw this supplement on TikTok..."

Supplements are a minefield of misinformation. Your role is to provide honest, evidence-based guidance on common fitness supplements while steering clients away from snake oil and always deferring to their healthcare provider for anything medical.

### The Conversation

**The universal supplement framework (use this every time):**

> "Supplements are exactly what the name implies — they supplement your diet. They don't replace good nutrition, and no supplement will fix a bad diet. Let's make sure your meal plan and your overall nutrition are solid first, and then we can talk about what might give you an additional edge."

**When asked about protein powder:**

> "Protein powder is one of the few supplements I'd call genuinely useful for most people. It's convenient, it's an efficient way to hit your protein target, and it's well-researched. That said, it's just food — it's dried protein in a convenient form. If you can hit your daily protein target from whole foods alone, you don't need it. If you're falling short, a scoop of whey or plant-based protein in a shake is a simple fix."

> "In your plan, I've included protein powder in [specific meal/snack]. If you'd prefer to get that protein from whole food instead, I can swap it out. Either way works."

**When asked about creatine:**

> "Creatine is the most studied supplement in sports nutrition, and the evidence is strong. It supports strength, power output, and recovery. For most people who are strength training, 3-5 grams of creatine monohydrate per day is a reasonable addition. It's affordable and well-tolerated."

> "That said — if you have any kidney concerns or medical conditions, check with your doctor first. And know that creatine causes a small amount of water retention, which can show up as a 1-2 pound increase on the scale. That's not fat — it's water in your muscles."

**When asked about fat burners:**

> "I'm going to be straight with you: most fat burners are overpriced caffeine pills with marketing behind them. The ones that do contain active ingredients often have side effects — elevated heart rate, anxiety, disrupted sleep — that aren't worth the marginal benefit. The most effective fat burner is a calorie deficit created through your meal plan and your training. That's not sexy, but it's what actually works."

**When asked about a trending supplement they saw online:**

> "Here's my filter for supplements: Is there peer-reviewed research supporting it? Has it been tested in humans (not just rats)? And does the benefit justify the cost and potential side effects? Most supplements that trend on social media don't pass that filter. Let me look into [specific supplement] and get back to you with what the research actually says. Deal?"

**When they want supplements for a medical purpose:**

> "That's moving into medical territory, and I want to make sure you get the right advice. Supplements for [medical purpose] should be recommended by your doctor or a registered dietitian who can look at your full health picture and any medications you're taking. What I can tell you is what your meal plan already includes in terms of [relevant nutrient], and we can make sure you're getting as much as possible from food first."

### Notes

- Stick to supplements with strong evidence: protein powder, creatine monohydrate, fish oil/omega-3s, vitamin D (in deficiency), caffeine (as a pre-workout). Beyond these, the evidence gets thin quickly.
- Never recommend a specific supplement brand in a way that sounds like a medical endorsement. "I personally use [brand]" is fine. "You should take [brand] to fix your [condition]" is not.
- If a client is spending significant money on supplements while not following their meal plan, address the priority: "I'd rather you spend that $150/month on quality food and follow your plan consistently than on supplements. The food is doing 95% of the work."
- Never sell supplements as part of your nutrition service unless you genuinely believe in the product and disclose any financial relationship. Clients can smell commission-driven recommendations from a mile away.

## Script 5: Nutrition Goal-Setting Conversations

### Context

A new client sits down for their nutrition consultation or first check-in. You need to help them set nutrition goals that are specific, realistic, and connected to their deeper motivations. Most clients come in with vague goals: "eat better," "lose weight," "be healthier." Your job is to turn those into concrete targets they can track and achieve.

This conversation also sets the tone for the entire coaching relationship. If you let them keep a vague goal, you'll both be directionless. If you push too hard for specificity, they'll feel interrogated. The sweet spot is curiosity and clarity.

### The Conversation

**Start with their words, not yours:**

> "Before we build your plan, I want to understand what success looks like for you. If we work together for the next 3 months and everything goes perfectly — what's different about your life? Don't give me the 'right' answer — give me the real one."

**Let them talk. Then probe deeper:**

If they say "I want to lose weight":

> "Okay, weight loss is the goal. Let's get specific. How much weight are you thinking? And more importantly — what does that weight loss mean to you? Is it about how you look, how you feel, fitting into certain clothes, having more energy, getting healthier numbers at the doctor? What's the real reason behind the number?"

If they say "I want to eat healthier":

> "I love that. What does 'healthier' mean to you specifically? Is it more vegetables? Less processed food? Less takeout? Better energy throughout the day? More structured meals? When you picture 'eating healthy,' what does a typical day look like in your mind?"

If they say "I want more energy":

> "Let's talk about that. When does your energy drop the most? Is it morning, mid-afternoon, after meals? And what does your current eating look like around those energy crashes? Often the energy issue is directly tied to meal timing and what you're eating at specific points in the day."

**Turn vague into specific:**

Once you have their real motivation, help them set a SMART nutrition goal:

> "Based on what you've told me, here's what I'd suggest as your primary nutrition goal for the next 90 days:"

**Examples of vague-to-specific conversions:**

| Vague Goal              | Specific Goal                                                                                                |
| ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| "Lose weight"           | "Lose 4-6kg over 12 weeks through a moderate calorie deficit while maintaining training performance"         |
| "Eat healthier"         | "Follow a structured meal plan 5 out of 7 days per week, with 4+ servings of vegetables daily"               |
| "More energy"           | "Eat a balanced meal within 60 minutes of waking and hit protein targets at every meal for the next 30 days" |
| "Build muscle"          | "Eat in a 300-calorie surplus with 1.6-2g protein per kg of body weight daily for the next 12 weeks"         |
| "Stop eating junk food" | "Reduce takeout meals from 5x/week to 2x/week over the next 4 weeks while following the meal plan"           |

**Set the tracking method:**

> "Here's how we'll measure progress: [tracking method]. We'll check in on this at our monthly reviews. If we're on track, we keep going. If something's not working, we adjust. The plan is a living document — it changes when you change."

**Address the mindset piece:**

> "One more thing — there will be days when you don't follow the plan. That's not failure. That's life. What I care about is the trend, not any single day. If you're hitting your targets 80% of the time, you're going to see results. So don't let one bad meal or one off day derail you. Just get back to the plan at your next meal."

**Close with commitment:**

> "So our goal for the next [timeframe] is [specific goal]. You're going to follow the meal plan I build for you, we'll check in [frequency], and we'll adjust as we go. On a scale of 1-10, how confident are you that you can commit to this? [If below 7, discuss what would need to change to raise their confidence — the obstacle they name is almost always the thing you need to address in the plan.]"

### Notes

- The "what does that mean to you" question is the most powerful tool in this conversation. The answer reveals whether a client is intrinsically motivated (they want to feel strong and energized) or extrinsically motivated (they want to look good for an event). Both are valid, but they require different coaching approaches.
- If a client's goal is unrealistic (e.g., "lose 20kg in 6 weeks"), address it honestly: "I want to set you up for success, not disappointment. A realistic and sustainable rate is [X] per week. At that rate, we're looking at [timeframe] to reach your goal. The upside is that weight lost at this rate stays off."
- Never promise specific outcomes. "You'll lose 10kg" is a promise. "Based on your plan and consistency, a healthy target is 0.5-1kg per week" is guidance. The difference matters legally and ethically.
- Document the goal. Write it in your client notes and reference it at every check-in. Clients who see their own goal in writing stay more accountable than clients who discussed it once and forgot.
- EvoFit Meals lets you set client-specific macro and calorie targets. Once the goal is set, configure the platform to match — so every plan generated aligns with the goal automatically.

## Quick Reference: The Scope Cheat Sheet

Keep this somewhere accessible. When in doubt, check it.

| Topic                | In Scope (You Can Advise)                               | Out of Scope (Refer Out)                                   |
| -------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| Macros and calories  | General targets for fitness goals                       | Therapeutic diets for medical conditions                   |
| Meal planning        | Custom plans for health and body composition            | Diets prescribed for diabetes, kidney disease, etc.        |
| Food choices         | Recommending whole foods, recipe suggestions            | Eliminating foods to treat allergies or medical conditions |
| Supplements          | General fitness supplements (protein, creatine)         | Supplements to treat medical conditions                    |
| Weight management    | Calorie deficit/surplus strategies for body composition | Weight management for eating disorders                     |
| Hydration            | General hydration guidelines                            | Fluid management for medical conditions                    |
| Meal timing          | Pre/post-workout nutrition, general meal spacing        | Therapeutic fasting for medical purposes                   |
| Emotional eating     | Recognising patterns and suggesting structure           | Diagnosing or treating eating disorders                    |
| Food intolerances    | Avoiding reported intolerances in meal plans            | Diagnosing food allergies or intolerances                  |
| Children's nutrition | General healthy eating guidance for parents             | Paediatric dietary therapy                                 |

**The golden rule:** If a client's question starts with "Can this food cure/treat/manage my [medical condition]?" — the answer is always "Let's get your doctor's input on that, and I'll build your plan around their recommendations."
