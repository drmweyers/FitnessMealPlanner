# Nutrition Retention & Re-engagement Kit

## Win-Back Scripts and Re-engagement Sequences for Clients Who Stopped Following Plans

---

### Why This Kit Exists

You will lose nutrition clients. Some will stop following their plans. Some will cancel. Some will simply go quiet and stop responding to messages. This is normal. The question isn't whether it will happen — it's whether you have a system for bringing them back.

The economics are clear: acquiring a new nutrition client costs 5-7x more than retaining an existing one. A client who returns after a 2-month gap is already onboarded, already trusts you, and already knows how your service works. Re-engagement is the highest-ROI activity in your business.

This kit gives you 5 win-back scripts for the most common departure scenarios, a client exit survey template, and a plan adjustment trigger checklist to help you catch at-risk clients before they leave.

---

## The 5 Re-engagement Scripts

### Script 1: The Dormant Client

**Scenario:** The client hasn't cancelled, but they've stopped responding to messages, missed their last check-in, and you suspect they're not following the plan. It's been 2-3 weeks of silence.

**Timing:** Send at the 2-week mark of no engagement. Don't wait longer — the further they drift, the harder the return.

**Message (text or email):**

> Hi [Name],
>
> I noticed we haven't connected in a couple of weeks and I wanted to reach out. No judgment, no pressure — life gets busy and I know nutrition isn't always the top priority when things pile up.
>
> I just want you to know two things:
>
> 1. Your plan is still active and I can update it anytime you're ready to pick things back up.
> 2. If something about the plan wasn't working — the recipes were too complicated, the portions didn't feel right, the timing didn't fit your schedule — I'd genuinely love to hear that. That's exactly the kind of feedback that lets me make the plan better for you.
>
> No need for a long reply. Even a thumbs up to let me know you're good would be great. And if now isn't the right time, that's okay too — I'll be here when you're ready.
>
> [Your Name]

**If they respond with an excuse (busy, stressed, fell off track):**

> "Totally understand. Here's what I'd suggest: let me rebuild your plan with a simplified version — fewer ingredients, faster recipes, and just 5 days instead of 7. We'll call it a reset week. Lower the bar, get a win, and build from there. Sound good?"

**If they don't respond after 5 more days:**

> "Hey [Name], just a final check-in from me. I'm going to pause your plan updates for now so you're not getting content you're not using. Whenever you want to restart — even if it's 3 months from now — just send me a message and I'll have a new plan ready within 48 hours. No re-onboarding needed. Take care."

**Then:** Move them to your "paused" list. Set a reminder to reach out again in 60 days.

### Script 2: The Plan-Abandoned Client

**Scenario:** The client explicitly told you they stopped following the plan. Maybe they said "I tried it for a week but couldn't stick to it" or "I just ended up eating whatever." They haven't cancelled yet, but they've mentally checked out.

**Timing:** As soon as they tell you. This is a conversation, not a message to send later.

**The Conversation:**

**Step 1: Acknowledge without judgment.**

> "Thanks for being honest with me — that actually takes guts, and most people just go quiet instead. Can I ask you something? What specifically made the plan hard to stick to?"

**Step 2: Listen. Really listen.** The answer usually falls into one of these categories:

| What They Say                      | What It Actually Means                                            |
| ---------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "The recipes were too complicated" | The plan didn't match their cooking ability or time               |
| "I didn't have time to meal prep"  | The plan required too much preparation for their lifestyle        |
| "I got bored of the food"          | Not enough variety, or the foods weren't things they enjoyed      |
| "I was still hungry"               | Portions were too small, or the plan didn't include enough volume |
| "My family eats differently"       | The plan didn't account for shared household meals                |
| "I just couldn't get started"      | Overwhelm — the change felt too big all at once                   |

**Step 3: Propose a specific fix.**

> "Okay, here's what I want to do. Based on what you've told me, I'm going to rebuild your plan with [specific change — e.g., simpler recipes, larger portions, family-compatible meals]. But instead of giving you a full 7-day plan, I'm going to give you just 3 days. Follow those 3 days. Tell me how it felt. Then we'll expand from there."

**Step 4: Reframe the goal.**

> "The goal isn't perfection — it's progress. If you follow the plan 3 out of 7 days this week, that's 3 more days of structured nutrition than you had before. We're building a habit, not flipping a switch. Let's start small and build."

**Step 5: Schedule immediate accountability.**

> "Let's check in on Friday. Not a formal session — just a quick text. Tell me how the 3 days went. That's it. Deal?"

### Script 3: The Plateau-Frustrated Client

**Scenario:** The client has been following the plan consistently but results have stalled. They're frustrated. They're questioning whether the plan works. They're on the edge of quitting because "nothing's happening anymore."

**Timing:** Address this the moment they express frustration. Don't wait for the scheduled check-in.

**The Conversation:**

**Step 1: Validate the frustration.**

> "I hear you, and I want you to know — what you're feeling is completely normal. Plateaus happen to everyone, and they're actually a sign that your body has adapted to the current approach. That's not a bad thing — it just means we need to adjust."

**Step 2: Investigate before changing anything.**

> "Before we change the plan, I want to understand what's happening. Can you walk me through a few things?"
>
> - "Are you still following the plan consistently — say, 80%+ of the time?"
> - "Has anything changed in your life recently — stress, sleep, activity level?"
> - "How are you measuring progress? Scale only, or are you also tracking measurements, photos, or how your clothes fit?"
> - "When did the plateau start?"

**Step 3: Explain what's likely happening.**

> "Plateaus usually happen for one of three reasons: your body has adapted to your current calorie level and needs a recalculation, your adherence has gradually drifted without you noticing, or you're measuring progress in a way that doesn't capture what's actually changing. Let me dig into which one it is for you."

**Step 4: Make a specific adjustment and set a new checkpoint.**

> "Here's what I'm going to do: I'm going to recalculate your macros based on where you are now — your body composition has likely changed since we started, which means your targets need to update. I'll also [adjust meal timing / increase protein / add a refeed day / modify carb cycling] based on what I'm seeing. Give me 48 hours to rebuild the plan, and let's commit to this new version for 3 weeks before we evaluate again."

**Step 5: Reframe the timeline.**

> "Results aren't linear. You'll see fast progress, then a stall, then another jump. The people who get the best long-term results are the ones who push through the plateaus instead of quitting during them. You're doing the hard part right now. Let's keep going."

### Script 4: The Holiday/Break Returner

**Scenario:** A client paused or cancelled before a holiday, vacation, or life event. They said they'd come back. It's been 4-8 weeks and they haven't. Or they're back and feeling guilty about how much they ate during the break.

**Timing:** Reach out at the 4-week mark if they haven't returned on their own. If they come back on their own, have this conversation at the first check-in.

**Outreach message (if they haven't returned):**

> Hi [Name],
>
> Hope [holiday/vacation/event] was incredible. I know you mentioned wanting to get back on track afterward — just wanted to check in and see where your head is at.
>
> No need to "start fresh" or feel like you're behind. Your profile, your preferences, and your last plan are all still saved in EvoFit Meals. I can have an updated plan ready for you within 24 hours of you saying "let's go."
>
> If you want to ease back in, I can build a simplified first week — lighter on prep, focused on getting the structure back. Just say the word.
>
> [Your Name]

**If they come back feeling guilty:**

> "First of all — welcome back. Second — whatever happened during [holiday/break] doesn't matter. It's done. There's no amount of guilt that changes what you ate last week, so let's drop it completely and focus on what's in front of us."
>
> "Here's the plan: I'm going to build you a reset week. It's not a punishment. It's a bridge. It's designed to get you back into the rhythm of structured eating without overwhelming you. Simpler meals, familiar recipes, reasonable portions. You follow it for 7 days, and by day 8 we're back to your regular plan. Sound good?"

**Key principles for the returner conversation:**

- Never make them feel bad about the break
- Never use phrases like "getting back on the wagon" (implies they fell off)
- Reduce friction for re-entry — make it feel easy
- Reference their previous progress: "Remember where you were at your best? We're going back there, and it'll happen faster this time because the foundation is already built."

### Script 5: The Budget-Concerned Client

**Scenario:** A client tells you they need to cut the nutrition service because of financial pressure. They might not want to cancel — they just feel like they can't justify the expense right now.

**Timing:** As soon as they raise the concern. Don't let this become a cancellation email.

**The Conversation:**

**Step 1: Respect the situation.**

> "I appreciate you telling me directly. Financial pressure is real and I'm not going to try to talk you into spending money you don't have. Let's figure out the best path forward."

**Step 2: Explore options before accepting the cancellation.**

> "Before we cancel, can I offer a couple of alternatives?"
>
> **Option A — Reduced service:** "I can move you to a lower-touch plan — a monthly plan instead of bi-weekly, with messaging support but no scheduled check-ins. That would bring the cost down to $[reduced price]. It's less hands-on, but you'd still have a structure to follow."
>
> **Option B — Pause:** "If this is a temporary crunch, I can pause your service for [1-2 months] at no charge. Your plan and preferences stay saved. When you're ready, we pick up right where we left off."
>
> **Option C — Transition pack:** "Let me build you a 4-week 'independent plan' — a plan designed to be followed without my active support. I'll include enough variety for a full month, all the recipes and shopping lists, and a self-assessment checklist so you can manage on your own for a while. If you want to come back later, the door is always open."

**Step 3: If they need to cancel, make it graceful.**

> "Understood. Here's what I'll do: your current plan stays active through the end of this billing cycle. I'll build one final plan that's designed to be sustainable without my support — simple recipes, repeatable meals, your macro targets for reference. And when your situation changes, just reach out. No re-onboarding, no awkward restart. We'll just pick up where we left off."

**Key principles:**

- Never make a client feel bad about money constraints
- Always offer a step-down before accepting a cancellation
- Make the exit graceful so the return is easy
- The transition pack is a powerful retention tool — many clients who receive one come back within 2-3 months because they realize they miss the support

**How to use exit survey data:**

Review your exit surveys monthly. Look for patterns:

| Pattern                                        | Action                                                                                             |
| ---------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Multiple clients citing "cost"                 | Consider adding a lower-price tier or adjusting your pricing                                       |
| Multiple clients citing "didn't fit lifestyle" | Review your onboarding — are you asking enough about their actual schedule and habits?             |
| Multiple clients citing "not seeing results"   | Review your check-in process — are you adjusting plans proactively?                                |
| Multiple clients citing "time"                 | Build more no-prep and quick-cook plan options                                                     |
| High "would return" scores (7+)                | Your service is good — the problem is external. Focus your re-engagement efforts on these clients. |

## Plan Adjustment Trigger Checklist

_Don't wait for clients to complain. Use this checklist at every check-in to catch issues before they become cancellation reasons._

### Check These at Every Monthly Review

**Adherence Signals:**

- [ ] Client followed the plan 80%+ of the time this month
  - If NO: Ask what got in the way. Adjust plan complexity, meal count, or prep time.

- [ ] Client completed their meal prep most weeks
  - If NO: Simplify the prep. Offer no-cook alternatives for some meals.

- [ ] Client is eating the meals they're supposed to (not consistently skipping or substituting)
  - If NO: Check which meals are being skipped. Swap them for something they prefer.

**Result Signals:**

- [ ] Client is making measurable progress toward their goal
  - If NO: Recalculate macros. Check if activity level has changed. Review adherence honestly.

- [ ] Client's weight trend is moving in the intended direction (or stable if maintaining)
  - If NO: Verify calorie targets. Check for untracked food intake. Consider a diet break if they've been in a deficit for 12+ weeks.

- [ ] Client reports acceptable energy levels and mood
  - If NO: Check if calories are too low. Review carb intake around training. Check sleep.

**Satisfaction Signals:**

- [ ] Client enjoys at least 70% of the recipes in their plan
  - If NO: Review food preference survey. Add more variety from their preferred cuisines.

- [ ] Client feels the plan is manageable within their schedule
  - If NO: Reduce cooking time per recipe. Cut the plan to fewer meals with more repeats.

- [ ] Client is engaged in check-ins (asking questions, providing feedback)
  - If NO: Red flag for disengagement. Initiate a casual check-in outside the scheduled time.

**Life Change Signals:**

- [ ] Has the client's schedule changed (new job, new routine, travel)?
  - If YES: Rebuild the plan around the new schedule immediately. Don't wait for them to ask.

- [ ] Has the client's training changed (new program, reduced frequency, injury)?
  - If YES: Recalculate calorie and macro targets. Adjust meal timing.

- [ ] Has the client mentioned financial stress, relationship stress, or major life events?
  - If YES: Check in more frequently. Simplify the plan. Reduce pressure.

- [ ] Has the client's household situation changed (new baby, partner's diet, kids' preferences)?
  - If YES: Adapt the plan for household compatibility. Include family-friendly recipes.

### The Traffic Light System

After each check-in, rate the client:

| Status | Meaning                                                        | Action                                                                         |
| ------ | -------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| GREEN  | Engaged, following plan, making progress                       | Continue as planned. Celebrate wins.                                           |
| AMBER  | Mixed adherence, some concerns, slight disengagement           | Proactive outreach within 48 hours. Adjust plan. Schedule an extra check-in.   |
| RED    | Not following plan, not responding, expressed desire to cancel | Immediate personal outreach. Offer plan rebuild, service adjustment, or pause. |

**Track your traffic light ratings in your client notes or in EvoFit Meals.** If a client moves from green to amber, act immediately. Don't wait to see if they recover on their own — they usually don't.

## The 90-Day Re-engagement Sequence

For clients who have cancelled, set up this automated (or manual) touchpoint sequence:

| Day After Cancellation | Action              | Message Focus                                                                        |
| ---------------------- | ------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Day 1                  | Exit survey         | Collect feedback                                                                     |
| Day 30                 | Check-in message    | "How's it going? Miss anything about structured eating?"                             |
| Day 60                 | Value-add           | Send a free seasonal recipe pack or nutrition tip relevant to their goal             |
| Day 90                 | Re-engagement offer | "I have a spot opening up — interested in a fresh start at a returning client rate?" |
| Day 180                | Long-game touch     | "Hey [Name], just thinking of you. How are things going?"                            |

**The 90-day message (template):**

> Hi [Name],
>
> It's been a few months and I wanted to reach out. I have a spot opening up in my nutrition coaching and I immediately thought of you.
>
> If you've been thinking about getting back on track, I'd love to work together again. For returning clients, I waive the setup process — your preferences and restrictions are all saved, and I can have a fresh plan ready within 24 hours.
>
> If now isn't the right time, no worries at all. But if it is — just reply and we'll get things moving.
>
> [Your Name]

**Expected return rate:** 15-25% of cancelled clients will re-engage within 6 months if you maintain the touchpoint sequence. That's significant — it means for every 10 clients you lose, you can expect 1-3 to return without any acquisition cost.
