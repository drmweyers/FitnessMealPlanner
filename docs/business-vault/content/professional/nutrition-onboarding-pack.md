# Nutrition Client Onboarding Pack

## Dietary Questionnaires, Food Preference Surveys, Allergy Forms, and Welcome Sequences

---

### What This Is

This is everything you need to onboard a new nutrition client professionally. Copy the templates, customize them for your brand, and use them every time a new client signs up. A structured onboarding process does three things: it collects the information you need to build an accurate plan, it sets expectations so the client knows exactly what to expect, and it signals professionalism that justifies your pricing.

Each template in this pack is ready to use. Adjust the wording to match your voice, add your branding, and send them out.

**How Your Plan Is Built**

Your meal plan is created using EvoFit Meals, a professional nutrition platform. Here's the process:

1. I review your questionnaire, preferences, and restrictions
2. I calculate your calorie and macro targets based on your goals, activity level, and body composition
3. I generate a plan that matches your preferences, cooking skills, and schedule
4. I review every plan personally before it reaches you — no generic output
5. You receive the plan with full recipes, portions, and a shopping list

If something doesn't work — a recipe you hate, an ingredient you can't find, a day that doesn't fit your schedule — tell me and I'll adjust it. The plan should serve you, not the other way around.

---

**How to Get the Best Results**

1. **Follow the plan for at least 5 full days before requesting changes.** Give your body and your routine a chance to adapt. Minor discomfort in the first few days (cravings, adjustment to new foods) is normal.

2. **Prep what you can.** You don't need to prep every meal, but having 2-3 days of lunches ready to go dramatically improves adherence. Your plan includes prep-friendly meals for this reason.

3. **Track your intake for the first 2 weeks.** Even rough tracking helps you build awareness of portions. After 2 weeks, most clients can eyeball portions accurately.

4. **Communicate honestly.** If you skip meals, eat off-plan, or have a rough week — tell me. I'm not here to judge. I'm here to adjust. A plan that accounts for real life works better than a perfect plan you can't follow.

5. **Ask questions.** There are no dumb questions in nutrition. If you don't understand why a food is in your plan, or you want to swap something, or you're confused about a recipe — ask.

## Template 8: Grocery Shopping Guide by Budget Tier

_For clients who are concerned about grocery costs. Tailor meal plans to their budget reality._

---

**Grocery Shopping Guide: Eating Well at Every Budget**

Your meal plan is built to be flexible across different budgets. Use this guide to make smart choices at the store without blowing your budget or sacrificing nutrition.

---

### Budget Tier 1: Tight Budget ($40-60/week for one person)

**Strategy:** Buy in bulk. Use frozen produce. Choose cheaper protein sources. Plan for leftovers.

**Best Protein Sources:**

- Eggs (the best value protein available: ~$0.25/egg, 6g protein each)
- Canned tuna ($1-2 per can, 20-25g protein)
- Chicken thighs (bone-in, skin-on: cheaper than breast, more flavor)
- Dried lentils and beans ($1-2 per bag, makes 6-8 servings)
- Cottage cheese (high protein per dollar)
- Ground turkey (buy in bulk, freeze portions)

**Best Carb Sources:**

- Rice (buy the biggest bag you can afford — cost per serving drops dramatically)
- Oats (bulk oats, not instant packets)
- Potatoes and sweet potatoes (one of the cheapest whole foods per calorie)
- Bananas (usually under $0.25 each)
- Bread (whole grain, store brand)

**Best Vegetable Sources:**

- Frozen vegetables (same nutrition as fresh, half the price, zero waste)
- Cabbage (one of the most nutrient-dense vegetables per dollar)
- Carrots (cheap, versatile, last for weeks)
- Canned tomatoes (base for dozens of recipes)
- Onions and garlic (flavor foundations that cost almost nothing)

**Budget Shopping Rules:**

1. Buy store brand — always. The nutrition is identical.
2. Check the unit price, not the sticker price. Bigger packages are almost always cheaper per serving.
3. Frozen produce is your best friend. It's picked at peak ripeness and flash-frozen. No waste.
4. Cook in batches. Making 4 servings of rice costs almost the same as making 1.
5. Avoid pre-cut, pre-washed, pre-seasoned anything. You're paying 2-3x for convenience.

---

### Budget Tier 2: Moderate Budget ($60-100/week for one person)

**Strategy:** Mix fresh and frozen. Add variety in protein. Include convenience items selectively.

**Upgrades from Tier 1:**

- Fresh chicken breast alongside thighs
- Salmon (frozen fillets — cheaper than fresh, same nutrition)
- Greek yogurt (bulk tubs, not individual cups)
- Fresh vegetables alongside frozen
- Variety of fruits (seasonal is cheapest)
- Olive oil and quality cooking fats

**Smart Splurges:**

- Pre-washed salad greens (saves prep time, reduces waste for single-portion salads)
- Nut butters (calorie-dense, versatile, lasts weeks)
- Protein powder (cost per gram of protein is competitive with whole food when you calculate it)

---

### Budget Tier 3: Comfortable Budget ($100-150+/week for one person)

**Strategy:** Prioritize quality, convenience, and variety. Buy organic where it matters. Use premium protein sources.

**Upgrades from Tier 2:**

- Fresh salmon and other fish varieties
- Grass-fed beef and organic chicken
- Full range of fresh produce (including organic "Dirty Dozen")
- Specialty items: quinoa, specialty grains, premium nuts
- Pre-made healthy convenience items (rotisserie chicken, pre-marinated proteins)

**The "Dirty Dozen" (buy organic if budget allows):**
Strawberries, spinach, kale, nectarines, apples, grapes, bell peppers, cherries, peaches, pears, celery, tomatoes

**The "Clean Fifteen" (conventional is fine):**

**Kitchen Setup Checklist: Everything You Need for Meal Prep**

You don't need a professional kitchen to follow your meal plan. Here's what you actually need, organized from essential to nice-to-have.

---

**Must-Have (you can't follow the plan without these):**

- [ ] **One good knife.** A chef's knife or santoku knife. This is the only kitchen tool that truly matters. A sharp knife makes everything faster and safer. If you buy one thing, buy this.
- [ ] **One cutting board.** Plastic or wood, large enough to chop vegetables comfortably.
- [ ] **One large non-stick pan.** For cooking proteins, stir-fries, eggs — 80% of your cooking happens here.
- [ ] **One large pot.** For boiling rice, pasta, soups, and batch-cooking grains.
- [ ] **Measuring cups and spoons.** For portion accuracy in the first few weeks. After that, you'll eyeball it.
- [ ] **A kitchen scale.** $10-15, and it's the most accurate way to portion proteins. Weighing your chicken breast takes 5 seconds and eliminates guesswork.
- [ ] **Food storage containers.** 10-15 containers in 2-3 sizes. Glass is better (microwave-safe, no staining), but plastic works fine. These are your meal prep lifeline.
- [ ] **A baking sheet.** For sheet-pan dinners, roasted vegetables, and baked proteins. Line with foil for zero cleanup.

**Strongly Recommended (makes meal prep significantly faster):**

- [ ] **A slow cooker or Instant Pot.** Set it and forget it. Bulk-cook proteins, soups, stews, and chili with almost zero active time. The Instant Pot is the single best purchase for time-pressed meal preppers.
- [ ] **A blender.** For smoothies, protein shakes, and sauces. Doesn't need to be expensive — a $30 blender handles everything your plan requires.
- [ ] **An air fryer.** Crisps food without oil. Great for quick proteins and vegetables. Not essential, but once you have one, you'll use it daily.

**Nice-to-Have (quality-of-life upgrades):**

- [ ] **A rice cooker.** If you eat rice frequently, this removes all guesswork. Set it and perfect rice every time.
- [ ] **Silicone spatulas and tongs.** For stirring, flipping, and serving without damaging non-stick surfaces.
- [ ] **A garlic press.** Saves 2 minutes every time a recipe calls for garlic. Small thing, big time saver.
- [ ] **Reusable zip-lock bags.** For freezing pre-portioned proteins and batch-cooked meals.

**You Do NOT Need:**

- A stand mixer (unless you're baking, which isn't in most meal plans)
- A food processor (a knife handles everything in your plan)
- An expensive knife set (one good knife beats a block of mediocre ones)
- A juicer (eat your fruit, don't drink it — fiber matters)
- Any single-purpose gadget you'd only use once a month
