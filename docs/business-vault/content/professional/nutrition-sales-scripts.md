# Nutrition Sales & Objection Scripts

## The 15 Conversations That Close Nutrition Coaching Clients

---

### Why Scripts Matter for Nutrition Sales

Selling nutrition coaching is harder than selling training sessions. With training, the client can see the gym, feel the workout, and picture themselves getting stronger. Nutrition is invisible. The results are slower. The resistance is deeper — because food is emotional, personal, and tangled up with identity, family, and years of failed diets.

That's exactly why you need frameworks for these conversations. Not word-for-word scripts you recite like a robot, but proven structures that help you navigate the most common pushback without losing your footing. Every script in this guide follows the same pattern:

1. **Acknowledge** — Show them you genuinely heard the concern. Never dismiss it.
2. **Reframe** — Shift the conversation from their past experience to your unique approach.
3. **Bridge** — Connect back to the specific health or body composition goal they told you about.
4. **Close or Advance** — Ask for the commitment, or set a concrete next step.

One more thing: this is not about pressure selling. Nutrition coaching only works when the client trusts you and opts in willingly. Your job in these conversations is to remove confusion, address real concerns, and help them make a decision that serves their goals. If the answer is genuinely no, let it be no — and leave the door open. The best nutrition clients often come back two months later when the thing they tried instead didn't work.

## Objection 1: "I Can Meal Prep Myself"

**Why they say this:** They think nutrition coaching is just someone handing them recipes. They've watched meal prep videos on YouTube, bought containers from Amazon, and maybe even had a good run for two or three weeks. They genuinely believe the only thing standing between them and results is a collection of recipes — and they can find those for free. What they don't see is the difference between having recipes and having a system that adapts to their life week after week.

**The Reframe:** Recipes are ingredients — a nutrition system is the architecture that makes those ingredients work for your body, your schedule, and your goals consistently.

**The Script:**

> **Client:** "I appreciate the offer, but honestly I can just meal prep on my own. There are tons of recipes online."
>
> **You:** "You're absolutely right — there are thousands of great recipes out there. And I'd bet you've probably tried meal prepping before, yeah?"
>
> **Client:** "Yeah, I've done it a few times. It went well for a couple weeks."
>
> **You:** "That's really common. Here's what I've noticed with almost every client I work with: the recipes were never the problem. The problem is what happens in week three when you're bored of chicken and rice, your schedule shifts, you eat out twice unexpectedly, and suddenly the whole plan falls apart. Does that sound familiar?"
>
> **Client:** "Yeah, actually. That's pretty much what happens."
>
> **You:** "That's exactly what I solve. I don't just hand you a list of meals. I build a system around your life — your schedule, your preferences, your training days versus rest days, even what your family eats. And when things go sideways — because they always do — I adjust the plan in real time instead of you having to start over from scratch. The platform has over 6,000 recipes with filters for every dietary need, so you'll never get bored and you'll never have to Google 'high protein dinner ideas' at 5 PM again. This isn't meal prep. It's meal architecture."
>
> **You:** "Want to see what a week built around your actual schedule looks like?"

**If They Still Say No:** "Totally fair. If you ever hit that wall in week three and want someone to build the system around you, I'm here. I'll check in with you in a few weeks."

## Objection 2: "Diets Don't Work for Me"

**Why they say this:** This one carries emotional weight. They've tried keto, paleo, Whole30, calorie counting, intermittent fasting — maybe all of them. Each time they lost weight, regained it, and ended up feeling worse than before. They're not just skeptical of your program. They're skeptical of themselves. The repeated failure has convinced them that their body is somehow different, that they're the exception. What they need to hear is that the diets failed them, not the other way around.

**The Reframe:** Diets are temporary restrictions with expiration dates — nutrition coaching is a permanent skill set that adapts with you.

**The Script:**

> **Client:** "Look, I've tried every diet out there. Nothing sticks. I don't think diets work for me."
>
> **You:** "Can I be honest? I actually agree with you. Diets don't work — not for you, not for most people. And that's not because something is wrong with you. It's because diets are designed to be temporary. They give you rigid rules, you white-knuckle through them for a few weeks, and the second real life shows up, the whole thing collapses."
>
> **Client:** "That's exactly what happens every time."
>
> **You:** "Right. So here's what's different about what I do: I'm not putting you on a diet. There's no food you can't eat. No phase one, phase two nonsense. What I build is a flexible nutrition framework around your actual life — your training schedule, your preferences, the foods you love, even the meals you eat out. We adjust it as we go based on how your body responds and what your week looks like."
>
> **You:** "Every plan is customized to your macros and your goals, and the system has dietary filters for literally any restriction or preference. So if you hate broccoli, broccoli is gone. If you love pasta, we work pasta in. The reason those other diets failed is because they asked you to live someone else's life. I'm building nutrition around yours."
>
> **You:** "Would it help if I showed you what a day of eating looks like when it's built around foods you actually enjoy?"

**If They Still Say No:** "I completely understand the hesitation. You've been burned before. If you ever want to try an approach that doesn't feel like a diet, reach out. I'll keep a spot open."

## Objection 3: "I Already Know What to Eat"

**Why they say this:** They probably do know, at least in broad strokes. Lean protein, vegetables, whole grains, less sugar — the basics aren't a mystery. But there's a canyon between knowing what to eat and consistently eating it in the right amounts, at the right times, adjusted for training days, recovery, and real-life chaos. This objection is really about pride — they don't want to admit that knowledge alone hasn't been enough.

**The Reframe:** Everyone knows what to eat. The gap between knowing and doing is where nutrition coaching lives.

**The Script:**

> **Client:** "I appreciate it, but I already know what I should be eating. I just need to be more disciplined."
>
> **You:** "I believe you. Honestly, most of my clients knew exactly what to eat before they started working with me. That wasn't what was missing."
>
> **Client:** "So what was missing?"
>
> **You:** "Execution. Structure. Accountability. Knowing that chicken and vegetables is a good dinner doesn't help you at 6 PM on a Wednesday when you're exhausted, the kids are hungry, and the easiest option is ordering takeout. What helps is having a plan already built for that exact scenario — a 20-minute meal that fits your macros, uses ingredients you bought on Sunday, and feeds the whole household."
>
> **You:** "That's what I build. Not education — implementation. Your plan is customized, it adjusts to your training schedule, and when life throws a curveball, we pivot the plan instead of abandoning it. Plus, I'm tracking your progress and adjusting your nutrition targets as your body changes. That's the piece that discipline alone can't replace."
>
> **You:** "Think of it this way: you know how to exercise too, but having a structured training program still makes a difference, right? Nutrition is the same. Knowing isn't the bottleneck — having a system is. Want me to build that system for you?"

**If They Still Say No:** "That's fair. If you find that discipline alone isn't getting you where you want to be, the door is always open. I'll follow up in a month to see how things are going."

## Objection 4: "It's Too Expensive on Top of Training"

**Why they say this:** This is a real concern for most clients. They're already paying for training sessions, and now you're suggesting they add nutrition coaching on top. It feels like the bill is stacking up. What they need to understand is that nutrition isn't an add-on — it's the multiplier that makes their training investment actually pay off. Without it, they're leaving 70% of their results on the table.

**The Reframe:** Nutrition coaching doesn't add to your training cost — it multiplies the return on the investment you're already making.

**The Script:**

> **Client:** "I'm already paying for training. Adding nutrition on top feels like a lot."
>
> **You:** "I totally understand that. You're already making a real investment in your health, and I respect that. Can I share something that might reframe this?"
>
> **Client:** "Sure."
>
> **You:** "You've probably heard the saying that results are 70% nutrition and 30% training. I don't know if those exact numbers are right, but directionally it's true — you cannot out-train a poor diet. Right now, you're investing in the 30%. The training is working. Your form is improving, you're getting stronger. But if the nutrition isn't dialed in, you're paying full price for 30% of the results."
>
> **You:** "Adding nutrition coaching doesn't double your cost — it completes the equation. It's the difference between working hard in the gym and actually seeing the body composition changes you're training for. Most of my clients who add nutrition see more visible progress in the first month than they saw in the previous three months of training alone."
>
> **You:** "And here's the thing — the meal plans are fully built for you through the platform. Customized to your macros, your dietary preferences, your schedule. You're not paying for generic PDFs. You're getting a system that runs alongside your training program and makes every session count. Want me to show you the cost per day? It's usually less than the coffee you're buying on the way to the gym."

**If They Still Say No:** "No pressure at all. Keep training, and when you feel ready to unlock the other 70%, I'm here. I'll mention it again when the time feels right."

## Objection 5: "My Partner/Family Won't Eat That"

**Why they say this:** This is a logistics concern disguised as an objection. They're imagining having to cook two separate meals every night — one "diet meal" for themselves and one "normal meal" for the family. They've probably tried this before with a restrictive diet and it was unsustainable. What they need to know is that a well-designed nutrition plan doesn't require a separate kitchen. It fits into the household.

**The Reframe:** A good nutrition plan doesn't isolate you at dinner — it feeds the whole table while still hitting your targets.

**The Script:**

> **Client:** "The problem is my family. My husband won't eat 'diet food' and my kids are picky. I'd have to cook two meals every night."
>
> **You:** "That's one of the most common concerns I hear, and it's completely valid. Cooking two separate meals every night is a non-starter — nobody can sustain that."
>
> **Client:** "Exactly. That's why every diet I've tried falls apart."
>
> **You:** "Here's the thing — I don't build meal plans that require you to eat in isolation. The platform has over 6,000 recipes, and a huge portion of them are family-friendly meals that your husband and kids will actually eat. We're talking things like taco bowls, stir-fries, pasta dishes, sheet-pan dinners — real food that happens to be portioned and balanced for your goals."
>
> **You:** "The difference is in the portions and maybe a side swap. Your family gets the full meal. You get the same meal, portioned to your macros, maybe with extra vegetables instead of a second helping of rice. Same dinner table. Same food. No separate cooking required."
>
> **You:** "I also build plans that account for the nights your family does takeout or eats something that doesn't fit your targets. We plan around those nights instead of pretending they won't happen. Want me to pull up some of the family-friendly recipes so you can see what I mean?"

**If They Still Say No:** "I get it — the family dynamic is real. If you want to try a one-week sample plan to see how it fits with your household, I'm happy to put that together. No commitment."

## Objection 6: "I Travel Too Much for a Meal Plan"

**Why they say this:** They're picturing a rigid meal plan that requires them to be in their kitchen with specific ingredients at specific times. Since they're on the road, in hotels, or eating out frequently, they've written off structured nutrition as incompatible with their lifestyle. What they don't realize is that a good nutrition system is designed for exactly this kind of unpredictability.

**The Reframe:** A nutrition plan that only works in your kitchen isn't a plan — it's a fantasy. Real plans travel with you.

**The Script:**

> **Client:** "I'm on the road three or four days a week. A meal plan just isn't realistic for my lifestyle."
>
> **You:** "How many days a week are you actually at home with access to your own kitchen?"
>
> **Client:** "Maybe three or four, depending on the week."
>
> **You:** "Perfect. So here's what I'd build: for the days you're home, you get structured meal plans — prepped, portioned, optimized for your goals. For the days you're traveling, I build a flexible framework: restaurant guidelines, fast-casual go-to orders, hotel room meal options, and macro targets you can hit with what's available wherever you are."
>
> **You:** "The platform lets me set up different day templates — home days and travel days — so your plan adapts to your actual week instead of pretending every day looks the same. I also include travel-friendly meal options that require zero cooking. Some of my busiest clients are travelers, and they see the best results because we've built a system that accounts for the chaos instead of ignoring it."
>
> **You:** "The biggest mistake people make with nutrition is assuming it only works under perfect conditions. I build plans for real conditions — your conditions. Want me to map out what a typical travel week would look like?"

**If They Still Say No:** "Totally understand. If you want to test-drive the travel framework for a week on your next trip, let me know. It might surprise you how manageable it is."

## Objection 7: "I'll Start Monday" / "After the Holidays"

**Why they say this:** This is a delay tactic, and it usually comes from one of two places: fear of commitment or the belief that they need a perfect starting condition. They're waiting for a Monday, a new month, the end of a vacation, post-holidays, after the birthday party — there's always something. The reality is that there will never be a clean starting line. The perfect time doesn't exist, and every week they wait is a week of results they won't get back.

**The Reframe:** There's no perfect Monday. The best nutrition plan starts in the middle of the mess — because that's where real life happens.

**The Script:**

> **Client:** "I love this, but the timing isn't great. Let me start after the holidays. I'll reach out in January."
>
> **You:** "I hear you. And honestly, that instinct makes total sense — start fresh, clean slate, new year energy. Can I push back just a little bit?"
>
> **Client:** "Sure."
>
> **You:** "The holidays are actually the perfect time to start. Not because I want you counting macros at Christmas dinner — that's not what this is. But because the clients who learn to navigate holidays, travel, parties, and social eating with a flexible plan are the ones who never fall off. If your nutrition system only works when life is calm and predictable, it's not a system. It's a fair-weather plan."
>
> **You:** "Here's what I'd do: we start now with a flexible framework. Through the holidays, the goal isn't perfection — it's building the skill of making better choices without restricting yourself. By January, while everyone else is starting from scratch, you'll already have momentum, habits, and a system that works."
>
> **You:** "Every week you wait is a week you could have been making progress. And I promise, there will never be a week with zero obstacles. So we might as well start learning to navigate them now. What if we kicked off this week with a simple baseline plan — nothing extreme?"

**If They Still Say No:** "No problem at all. I'm going to check in with you on January 2nd. And when I do, let's not wait for another Monday. Deal?"

## Objection 8: "I Have Too Many Restrictions/Allergies"

**Why they say this:** They've had bad experiences with generic meal plans that ignore their dietary needs. Maybe they're celiac, lactose intolerant, vegetarian, or managing multiple food sensitivities. They've received plans before that were useless on day one because half the meals contained foods they can't eat. They assume your system will be the same — one-size-fits-all with no room for their complexity.

**The Reframe:** Your restrictions aren't a problem to work around — they're the blueprint I use to build your plan.

**The Script:**

> **Client:** "I don't think this would work for me. I'm gluten-free, dairy-free, and I can't have soy. Every meal plan I've tried ignores at least one of those."
>
> **You:** "That's incredibly frustrating. Let me guess — you've gotten plans where you had to substitute half the meals yourself, which basically meant the plan was useless?"
>
> **Client:** "Yes. Every single time."
>
> **You:** "That won't happen here. The system I use has built-in dietary filters for every restriction you just named — and dozens more. When I build your plan, gluten, dairy, and soy are excluded before I even start selecting recipes. Every single meal in your plan will be safe for you from day one. No substitutions required."
>
> **You:** "The platform has over 6,000 recipes across every dietary category you can think of: gluten-free, dairy-free, soy-free, nut-free, vegan, keto, paleo — and combinations of all of them. Your restrictions actually make my job more focused, not harder. I'm not trying to fit you into a template. I'm building a template around you."
>
> **You:** "On top of that, if you have a reaction to something or discover a new sensitivity, I update the plan immediately. No waiting for next month's meal plan. We adjust it that day. Want me to show you how many recipes are available with your specific combination of filters? The number might surprise you."

**If They Still Say No:** "Completely understand. If you ever get tired of piecing together your own meals around your restrictions, this is literally what the system was built for. I'll be here."

## Objection 9: "Our Company Already Has a Wellness Program"

**Why they say this:** You are pitching corporate wellness nutrition services and the decision-maker is telling you they've already ticked the "wellness" box. They probably have a gym discount, maybe an annual health screening, or a generic wellness app. What they almost certainly don't have is individualized nutrition coaching that actually changes employee behavior and drives measurable health outcomes.

**The Reframe:** A gym discount is a benefit. Personalized nutrition coaching is a performance intervention that reduces sick days, boosts energy, and delivers measurable ROI.

**The Script:**

> **Decision-Maker:** "We already offer wellness benefits. Our employees get a gym membership discount and we have a wellness app."
>
> **You:** "That's great — you're already investing in your team's health. Can I ask: what percentage of your employees actually use those benefits regularly?"
>
> **Decision-Maker:** "Honestly, probably 15-20%."
>
> **You:** "That's actually pretty typical. The challenge with passive benefits — gym discounts, app access, wellness portals — is that they require the employee to be self-motivated. The 15% who use them were probably already active. The other 85% need something more structured, more personal, and more directly tied to their daily life. That's where nutrition coaching fits."
>
> **You:** "What I offer is a nutrition program your employees can actually engage with — customized meal plans built for their individual goals, dietary needs, and schedules. Not a generic app. Not a pamphlet about eating more vegetables. A real plan with real meals, grocery lists, and ongoing coaching support. The engagement rates I see with my corporate clients are 50-65% — three to four times higher than passive wellness tools."
>
> **You:** "And here's the business case: employees who eat well have more energy, fewer sick days, and better focus. A University of Warwick study found that well-nourished, healthy employees are 13% more productive. For a team of 50, even a 5% productivity gain pays for the program multiple times over."
>
> **You:** "Would it make sense to run a 30-day pilot with one department? I'll show you the engagement data and the employee feedback at the end, and you can decide whether to scale from there."

**If They Still Say No:** "I understand — budgets and priorities are real. If you're interested, I can send you a one-page overview of the corporate program and the ROI framework. When wellness renewals come up, you'll have a stronger option on the table."

## Objection 10: "I'd Rather Just Do Group Classes"

**Why they say this:** You are pitching a group nutrition class or program, and the prospect is comparing it to a generic group fitness class — spin, yoga, bootcamp. They don't see how a nutrition group is different, or they think they can just YouTube their way through nutrition education. What they don't understand is that a guided group nutrition program provides structure, accountability, community, and individualized attention within a group format.

**The Reframe:** Group fitness builds your body. Group nutrition builds the habits that determine whether your body actually changes.

**The Script:**

> **Prospect:** "I already do group classes at the gym. I don't think I need a nutrition group too."
>
> **You:** "I love that you're active — group classes are great for fitness. Can I ask you something? How are your results? Are you seeing the body changes you want from those classes?"
>
> **Prospect:** "Honestly, I feel like I've plateaued."
>
> **You:** "That's extremely common. And here's why: group fitness handles the exercise side, but it doesn't touch the nutrition side. And nutrition is where body composition actually changes. You can burn 500 calories in a spin class and undo it with one post-workout smoothie that has 600 calories of sugar in it. It's not about working harder — it's about aligning your eating with your effort."
>
> **You:** "My group nutrition program gives you the same energy as a group class — community, accountability, expert guidance — but applied to the thing that's actually stalling your results. You'll get a customized meal plan through EvoFit Meals, weekly group coaching sessions where we cover real-life nutrition challenges, and a community of people going through the same thing. It's not a lecture. It's a working session where you walk away with action items every week."
>
> **You:** "Think of it this way: your group fitness class is the engine. My nutrition group is the fuel system. The engine is only as powerful as the fuel you put in. Want to join our next cohort?"

**If They Still Say No:** "No worries. If your plateau is still frustrating you in a month, come try one session on me. No commitment."

## Objection 11: "I'm Already Working with a Trainer — You're Not My Trainer"

**Why they say this:** You are pitching nutrition services to someone who trains with another trainer — either from a referral, a cold DM, or a partnership lead. They feel loyalty to their current trainer, or they think it's strange to get nutrition from someone who isn't also training them. They need to understand that nutrition coaching is a standalone discipline, and working with a nutrition specialist alongside their trainer is the optimal setup — not a conflict.

**The Reframe:** Your trainer handles the training. I handle the nutrition. Together, you get 100% of the results instead of 30%.

**The Script:**

> **Prospect:** "I already have a trainer, and I feel like adding a separate nutrition coach would be weird."
>
> **You:** "That's actually the ideal setup. Think about it this way: your trainer is an expert at programming your workouts, pushing you in sessions, and managing your physical progress. That's their specialization. What I specialize in is the nutrition side — building meal plans that support your training goals, adjusting your fuel based on your training volume, and making sure the work you do in the gym actually translates to the body composition changes you want."
>
> **You:** "Having both isn't redundant. It's synergistic. Your trainer designs the stimulus. I design the recovery and fuel. When those two are coordinated, results accelerate dramatically. I've worked with clients whose trainers have told me it was like 'unlocking a level' — the client started progressing faster because their nutrition finally matched their training."
>
> **You:** "And I'm happy to coordinate with your trainer. I'll share your nutrition targets with them so they know what your fuel looks like, and they can share your training schedule with me so I can time your carbs and protein around your sessions. The client who has a trainer AND a nutrition coach is the client who gets the best results. Period."
>
> **You:** "Would you be open to a free consultation? I'll show you what a week of nutrition looks like when it's actually designed to support your training."

**If They Still Say No:** "Completely respect that. If your trainer ever mentions that nutrition might be the missing piece, have them reach out to me directly. I work with several trainers as a nutrition partner."

## Objection 12: "I Tried an Online Nutrition Coach Before and It Was Terrible"

**Why they say this:** They got burned. Maybe they paid for a "custom" plan that was clearly a template. Maybe the coach disappeared after sending the first plan and never followed up. Maybe they got a meal plan full of foods they hate, or the coach used a cookie-cutter approach that ignored everything they said in their intake form. This objection is about trust — they don't trust that your service will be different.

**The Reframe:** A bad experience with one coach doesn't disqualify all coaching — it just raised your standards. Let me meet them.

**The Script:**

> **Prospect:** "I tried working with an online nutrition coach before. It was a waste of money."
>
> **You:** "I'm sorry to hear that. Unfortunately, that's not uncommon in this industry. Can I ask — what specifically went wrong?"
>
> **Prospect:** [describes their bad experience — template plan, no communication, wrong foods, no adjustments, etc.]
>
> **You:** "That's frustrating, and I completely understand why you'd be hesitant to try again. Here's what I can tell you about how I work: [directly address each complaint they raised]."

**Address the most common bad-experience complaints:**

If the plan was a template:

> "Every plan I build starts with a detailed questionnaire about your goals, preferences, allergies, schedule, cooking ability, and household. Your plan is built inside EvoFit Meals using 6,000+ recipes filtered specifically for you. No templates. If you ever receive a meal that doesn't fit, I swap it within hours."

If the coach ghosted:

> "I run a structured check-in schedule — you'll hear from me at minimum once per week, usually more. Mid-week touchpoints, adherence check-ins, and a full monthly review. If I ever go quiet, I want you to call me out. Communication is the service."

If the plan was never adjusted:

> "Your plan changes as you change. I review your progress data, adjust macros, rotate recipes, and recalibrate targets on a regular cycle. A static plan is not coaching — it's a document. What I offer is an evolving system."

**Then close with a risk reversal:**

> "Here's what I'd suggest: let me build you a free 3-day sample plan based on your actual preferences and goals. You'll see the quality of the plan, the level of customization, and how the platform works before you spend a cent. If it feels like the same thing you got before — walk away. If it feels different — and I'm confident it will — we'll talk about getting started. Fair?"

**If They Still Say No:** "No pressure at all. You've earned the right to be cautious. If you change your mind, just reach out. I'll still be here."

## Objection 13: "Can You Just Send Me a PDF?"

**Why they say this:** They want the result without the relationship. They're thinking of nutrition coaching as a transaction — pay, receive document, done. This might be because they've never experienced real coaching, or because their budget is tight and they want the minimum viable product. Either way, you need to decide: do you sell a one-off plan (which is a valid product), or do you educate them on why the coaching component is where the value lives?

**The Reframe:** A PDF is a snapshot. Coaching is a system that adapts, responds, and evolves with you.

**The Script:**

> **Prospect:** "I don't really need ongoing coaching. Can you just send me a meal plan and I'll follow it?"
>
> **You:** "Absolutely — I do offer one-off custom meal plans. Before we go that route, can I share one thing I've learned from doing this?"
>
> **Prospect:** "Sure."
>
> **You:** "The clients who get the best results aren't the ones who get the best plan on day one. They're the ones whose plan gets better every week because it's being adjusted. A one-off plan works great for the first 2-3 weeks. But then your body adapts, your schedule changes, you get bored of certain meals, or you hit a plateau — and the PDF doesn't have answers for any of that."
>
> **You:** "The ongoing coaching piece is where I recalculate your macros as your weight changes, swap out recipes you're tired of, adjust for travel or holidays, and keep the plan aligned with your progress. The plan on Week 8 is very different from the plan on Week 1 — because you're different."
>
> **You:** "That said, I understand if ongoing coaching isn't where you want to start. Here's what I'd suggest: let me build you a one-off plan. Follow it for 2-3 weeks. If you find yourself wanting adjustments, support, or a refresh — that's when the monthly coaching becomes a no-brainer. Most of my one-off clients upgrade within 30 days because they see the gap."

**If They Insist on Just the PDF:** "Let's do it. I'll build you a great plan. And if you want the coaching later, it's always an option."

## Objection 14: "I'm Not Sure You're Qualified — You're a Trainer, Not a Nutritionist"

**Why they say this:** They're questioning your credentials. This is either genuine skepticism (they want to know you have the knowledge) or it's a negotiating tactic (they're looking for a reason to say no). Either way, it needs to be addressed directly and confidently. This is not the time to be defensive or to overstate your credentials. Honesty and clarity win here.

**The Reframe:** Credentials matter. So does specialization. I don't treat medical nutrition conditions — I build meal plans for fitness and body composition. That's exactly what you need.

**The Script:**

> **Prospect:** "No offense, but you're a personal trainer. Shouldn't I be seeing a nutritionist or dietitian for this?"
>
> **You:** "Great question, and I'm glad you asked. You're right to care about qualifications. Let me be clear about what I do and what I don't do."
>
> **You:** "If you have a medical condition that requires dietary management — diabetes, eating disorders, kidney disease, clinical-level food allergies — then yes, a registered dietitian is the right call, and I'd refer you to one without hesitation. I actually keep a referral list for exactly that situation."
>
> **You:** "But if your goal is fat loss, muscle building, better energy, or just eating in a way that supports your fitness — that's firmly within my scope. I hold [your certification], I use a professional nutrition platform with 6,000+ recipes and precise macro calculations, and I've helped [X] clients achieve measurable results through structured meal planning."
>
> **You:** "Think of it this way: a dietitian is a doctor for your diet — they handle the clinical side. I'm the architect — I build practical, day-to-day nutrition systems that align with your training and your life. Many of my clients work with both — a dietitian sets the parameters, and I build the plan within those parameters."
>
> **You:** "Would it help if I shared some results from clients with goals similar to yours?"

**If They Still Say No:** "I appreciate the honesty. If you'd like, I can refer you to a registered dietitian I work with. And if you ever want the practical meal planning side handled, that's where I come in."

## Objection 15: "I Follow a Fitness Influencer Who Gives Free Nutrition Advice"

**Why they say this:** They're consuming free nutrition content — from Instagram reels, YouTube videos, TikTok fitness influencers — and they believe that content is sufficient. They're comparing your paid service to free content and wondering why they'd pay for something they can get for free. What they don't understand is that content is education, not implementation. It tells you what to do in general. It doesn't build a system for your life in particular.

**The Reframe:** Free content tells you what to eat. Coaching builds the system that makes you actually do it — customized for your body, your schedule, and your goals.

**The Script:**

> **Prospect:** "I follow this fitness influencer who posts tons of nutrition advice. I feel like I can just follow their content."
>
> **You:** "Nice — who do you follow? [Let them share.] I've seen their stuff — some of it is actually pretty solid. Can I ask you something though?"
>
> **Prospect:** "Sure."
>
> **You:** "How long have you been following their advice, and how much has your body actually changed in that time?"
>
> **Prospect:** [Usually admits: not much, or they haven't been consistent.]
>
> **You:** "That's the gap. The content is fine. The problem is that it's generic. When an influencer posts 'here's my full day of eating at 2,400 calories,' that's their day of eating for their body, their goals, their training schedule, and their preferences. You're not them. Your calorie target is different. Your macros are different. Your food preferences are different. Your schedule is different."
>
> **You:** "What I do is take all the principles that good nutrition content teaches and turn them into a custom implementation plan for you. Specific meals. Specific portions. Specific recipes filtered for your dietary needs. A grocery list for your week. And ongoing adjustments as your body changes. An influencer can't do that for you — they're talking to a million people. I'm building for one person: you."
>
> **You:** "Think of it this way: watching workout videos doesn't replace having a personal trainer. Watching nutrition content doesn't replace having a personal nutrition coach. The value isn't in the information — it's in the personalization and the accountability. Want me to show you what the personalized version looks like?"

**If They Still Say No:** "Keep learning from them — good content is valuable. And if you ever want to turn that knowledge into an actual system built for your body, reach out."

### A Note on Trust Over Pressure

The best nutrition clients are the ones who choose to work with you — not the ones you talked into it. These scripts are designed to remove confusion and address real concerns, not to manipulate someone into a purchase they'll regret.

If a client says no after you've genuinely addressed their objection, respect it. Send them a follow-up in two to four weeks. Stay visible. Share nutrition tips. Be the person they think of when they're finally ready. The long game in nutrition coaching is trust, and trust compounds. A client who comes back to you on their own terms will stay longer, comply better, and refer more people than someone you pressured into signing up.

Sell with clarity. Close with confidence. But always leave the door open.
