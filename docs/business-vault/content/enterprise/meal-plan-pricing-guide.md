# Meal Plan Pricing & Packaging Guide

## How to Price Nutrition Coaching, Build Team Rate Sheets, Forecast Revenue, and Create Enterprise Contract Templates

---

### Before You Read This

Most trainers who add nutrition services either undercharge dramatically (because they feel like meal plans "aren't worth that much") or price randomly (because they copied someone on Instagram without understanding the math). Both lead to the same place: frustration, burnout, and eventually dropping the service.

At the enterprise level, the pricing challenge compounds. You are not just pricing for one coach and a handful of clients. You are building rate sheets for a team, projecting revenue across multiple locations, calculating profit margins by service type, and negotiating contract pricing with corporate wellness buyers and franchise partners. Gut-feel pricing doesn't work here. You need a system.

This guide walks you through the individual pricing calculator, regional benchmarks, three ready-to-use package templates, pricing psychology, recurring revenue architecture, team and studio rate sheets, revenue forecasting models, profit margin analysis, ROI calculators, enterprise contract templates, and volume discount frameworks.

## Section 1: The Pricing Calculator

### Step 1: Know Your Time Investment Per Client

Before you set a price, you need to know what each nutrition client actually costs you in time.

**Initial setup (first week):**

| Task                                   | Time           |
| -------------------------------------- | -------------- |
| Review dietary questionnaire and forms | 15-20 min      |
| Calculate macros and calorie targets   | 10-15 min      |
| Build meal plan in EvoFit Meals        | 15-30 min      |
| Review and customize plan              | 10-15 min      |
| Deliver plan and explain it to client  | 15-20 min      |
| **Total initial setup**                | **65-100 min** |

**Monthly ongoing:**

| Task                                     | Time           |
| ---------------------------------------- | -------------- |
| Plan updates/adjustments (2-4 per month) | 20-40 min      |
| Monthly check-in call                    | 20-30 min      |
| Responding to questions/messages         | 15-30 min      |
| Progress review and macro adjustment     | 10-15 min      |
| **Total monthly maintenance**            | **65-115 min** |

So a nutrition client costs you roughly 1-2 hours per month after the initial setup. That is the denominator in your pricing equation.

### Step 2: Calculate Your Minimum Hourly Value

You already know your training session rate. Use that as your benchmark.

**If your training rate is $80/session (60 minutes):** Your minimum hourly value is $80.

**If your nutrition work takes 1.5 hours/month per client:** Your minimum nutrition price is $80 x 1.5 = **$120/month**.

That is your floor. Go below it and you are earning less per hour for nutrition work than for training — which means every hour spent on nutrition is an hour you could have earned more training someone.

### Step 3: Add Your Value Premium

Your floor is based on time. But nutrition services deliver value beyond your time:

- **The plan itself has intellectual value.** You calculated macros, selected recipes, built a structure. That knowledge and customization is worth something independent of the minutes it took.
- **Convenience value.** The client doesn't have to research, plan, shop, or figure anything out. You've eliminated hours of their weekly decision-making.
- **Outcome value.** Better nutrition leads to better results, which leads to longer client retention, which leads to more training revenue. The nutrition service makes your training service more valuable.

Add 25-50% above your floor to account for this value.

**Using the example above:** $120 floor x 1.35 = **$162/month** -> round to **$165 or $175/month**

### Step 4: Validate Against Your Market

Before finalizing, check what others in your area and niche are charging for nutrition services. Here's how:

1. Search "[your city] nutrition coaching" and note the top 5 prices
2. Check what online nutrition coaches in your niche charge
3. Ask in trainer communities what others charge for meal plans
4. Look at nutrition apps and meal plan delivery services for the "automated" baseline

Your price should be above the automated services (which charge $10-30/month) and competitive with local nutrition coaching (which ranges widely).

### Factors That Push Your Price Up

- Nutrition certification (Precision Nutrition, ISSA Nutrition, NASM CNC)

- Integration with training program (synced periodization)

### Factors That Push Your Price Down

- No nutrition-specific credential
- Template-based plans with minimal customization
- No ongoing support or check-ins
- Early-stage business with limited testimonials
- High competition in local market

## Section 3: Three Package Templates

### Package 1: The One-Off Meal Plan

**What it is:** A single, customized meal plan delivered as a one-time purchase. No ongoing coaching, no monthly check-ins. The client gets their plan and runs with it.

**Best for:** Clients on a budget, clients who want to "try before they commit," or as a lead-in product for your monthly service.

**What's included:**

- Initial questionnaire and preference review
- 7-day customized meal plan (built in EvoFit Meals)
- Full recipes with portions and macro breakdowns
- Consolidated shopping list
- Meal prep guide for the week
- One round of revisions within 7 days

**Pricing formula:** Your hourly rate x 2 hours (average time to build and deliver)

**Example pricing:**

- Small market: $100 - $150
- Mid market: $150 - $250
- Major metro: $200 - $350

### Package 2: Monthly Nutrition Service

**What it is:** Ongoing customized meal plans with regular updates and a monthly check-in. This is your bread-and-butter nutrition product.

**What's included:**

- Initial questionnaire, preference survey, and allergy review
- Bi-weekly customized meal plans (new plan every 2 weeks)
- Full recipes, macros, and shopping lists with every plan
- One 30-minute monthly nutrition check-in (video, phone, or in-person)
- Messaging support for quick questions (24-hour response time)
- Macro adjustments based on progress
- Plan swaps and modifications as needed

**Pricing formula:** Your hourly rate x 1.5 hours/month x 1.35 value premium

**Example pricing:**

- Small market: $100 - $175/month
- Mid market: $150 - $275/month
- Major metro: $200 - $400/month

### Package 3: Training + Nutrition Bundle

**What it is:** Your training package plus integrated nutrition coaching. One price, one relationship, comprehensive results.

**What's included (nutrition portion):**

- Everything in the Monthly Nutrition Service
- Plus: nutrition plan synced with training program
- Plus: nutrition check-in integrated into training sessions
- Plus: performance-focused meal timing recommendations

**Pricing formula:** Training package price + nutrition add-on (60-80% of standalone nutrition price)

## Section 4: Pricing Psychology

### The Anchor Effect

Always present your packages in this order:

1. **One-off plan** (lowest price, least value)
2. **Monthly service** (middle price, best value for most clients) — **this is the one you want them to buy**
3. **Training + nutrition bundle** (highest price, maximum value)

**Never present just one option.** A single option creates a yes/no decision. Three options create a "which one?" decision. "Which one?" converts at a dramatically higher rate.

### Price Presentation Rules

**Rule 1: Always quote the package price, not the per-meal price.**

**Rule 2: State the price once, confidently, and move immediately to value.**

**Rule 3: Never apologize for your price.**

**Rule 4: Use "investment," not "cost."**

### The Founding Member Play

When launching your nutrition service for the first time, a founding member offer creates urgency and rewards early adopters. Discount no more than 20-25% off your standard rate. Cap at 5-10 spots. Set a 2-3 week deadline.

## Section 5: Recurring Revenue Architecture

### Why Recurring Beats One-Off

**Monthly model compounding example:**

- Month 1: 4 clients at $150/month = $600
- Month 2: 4 existing + 3 new = 7 clients = $1,050
- Month 3: 7 existing + 3 new = 10 clients = $1,500
- Month 6: ~18 clients (assuming 85% retention) = $2,700
- Month 12: ~28 clients = $4,200

### The Retention Equation

| Factor                                 | Impact                          |
| -------------------------------------- | ------------------------------- |
| Regular check-ins                      | +40% retention vs. no check-ins |
| Visible results within 30 days         | +50% retention                  |
| Plan variety (new recipes regularly)   | +25% retention                  |
| Responsive communication               | +30% retention                  |
| Training integration (bundled clients) | +60% retention                  |

### Building Your Recurring Revenue Dashboard

| Metric                            | Formula                                     | Target                   |
| --------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------ |
| Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR)   | Sum of all active monthly nutrition clients | Growing month-over-month |
| Client count                      | Total active nutrition clients              | Growing                  |
| Average Revenue Per Client (ARPC) | MRR / client count                          | $125+                    |
| Churn rate                        | Clients lost / clients at start of month    | Under 15%                |
| Net new clients                   | New clients - churned clients               | Positive                 |
| Lifetime Value (LTV)              | ARPC x average client tenure (months)       | $750+                    |

## Section 6: Team/Studio Rate Sheets

**This section is for gym owners and program directors building a standardized pricing structure for a team of nutrition coaches.**

### Why Standardized Rates Matter

When every coach on your team sets their own prices, three problems emerge:

1. Clients comparison-shop between coaches within your own facility
2. Your brand positioning becomes incoherent — one coach charges $75 and another charges $200 for the same service
3. Revenue forecasting becomes impossible

Standardized rate sheets solve all three problems. Every coach charges the same rate. The brand is consistent. Revenue is predictable.

### Team Rate Sheet Template

| Service                              | Client Price | Coach Payout  | Business Revenue | Margin |
| ------------------------------------ | ------------ | ------------- | ---------------- | ------ |
| One-Off Meal Plan                    | $150         | $75 (50%)     | $75              | 50%    |
| Monthly Meal Plan (basic)            | $125/mo      | $50/mo (40%)  | $75/mo           | 60%    |
| Monthly Nutrition Coaching           | $175/mo      | $70/mo (40%)  | $105/mo          | 60%    |
| Training + Nutrition Bundle (add-on) | $100/mo      | $40/mo (40%)  | $60/mo           | 60%    |
| Premium Coaching (weekly check-ins)  | $300/mo      | $120/mo (40%) | $180/mo          | 60%    |
| Group Nutrition (per participant)    | $75/mo       | $25/mo (33%)  | $50/mo           | 67%    |

**Coach compensation model:** 40% of nutrition revenue to the coach is industry standard for gym-employed coaches. If coaches are independent contractors, 50-60% is typical. Adjust based on whether the business provides the platform subscription, marketing, and client acquisition.

### Multi-Location Rate Consistency

For multi-location operations, maintain identical pricing across all locations with one exception: if you operate in dramatically different markets (e.g., Manhattan and a small suburban town), create a maximum of two pricing tiers:

| Tier     | Locations                      | Pricing Adjustment         |
| -------- | ------------------------------ | -------------------------- |
| Metro    | High cost-of-living locations  | Standard rate              |
| Regional | Lower cost-of-living locations | Standard rate minus 15-20% |

Never create more than two tiers. The administrative overhead of managing three or more pricing tiers across multiple locations is not worth the marginal revenue optimization.

## Section 11: Volume Discount Frameworks

**When to offer volume discounts and how to structure them without eroding your margins.**

### The Golden Rules of Volume Discounts

1. **Never discount below your cost floor.** Calculate your minimum price per client (coach cost + platform cost + overhead allocation) and never go below it, regardless of volume.

2. **Discount the rate, not the service.** The client gets the same quality plan and coaching at a lower rate — you never reduce the service to hit a lower price point.

3. **Require commitment in exchange for discount.** Volume discounts are earned through longer commitments or guaranteed minimums, not given upfront on hope.

4. **Cap your maximum discount at 25%.** Beyond 25% off your standard rate, you are better off saying no. The client who negotiates you down 40% will be the first to complain about the quality they paid a premium for.

**For individual clients purchasing multiple months:**

**For B2B clients purchasing multiple seats:**

| Seats  | Discount      | Notes                                        |
| ------ | ------------- | -------------------------------------------- |

| 11-25  | 10%           | Requires 3-month minimum commitment          |

### Handling Discount Requests

When a prospect asks for a discount, don't immediately concede. Use this framework:

> **Prospect:** "Can you do better on the price?"
>
> **You:** "The rate I quoted reflects the full service — customized plans, coaching, ongoing adjustments, and platform access. I can offer a reduced rate if you're willing to commit to a longer term. For a 6-month commitment, I can bring it down to $[X]/month. That locks in your rate and guarantees your spot. Would that work?"

If they push further:

> "I want to be honest — I'd rather deliver a great service at this rate than cut corners to hit a lower number. What I can do is start with a 30-day trial at the standard rate, and if you're happy with the results, we'll discuss commitment options that come with a discount."

## Section 12: Common Pricing Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Pricing Based on How Long the Plan Takes to Generate

With EvoFit Meals, you can generate a complete meal plan in minutes. That doesn't mean the plan is worth "5 minutes of work." Price on value, not on production time.

### Mistake 2: Matching App Prices

You are not an app. Don't address the comparison unless the client brings it up.

### Mistake 3: Free Nutrition "Included" with Training

This devalues the service and makes it impossible to charge for it later.

### Mistake 4: Annual Discounts That Kill Cash Flow

Offer modest 3-month or 6-month discounts (10-15%) instead of large annual ones.

### Mistake 5: Not Raising Prices

Review and raise prices every 12 months. Existing clients get 30 days notice and a loyalty lock-in option.

### Mistake 6: Inconsistent Pricing Across Coaches (Enterprise)

Every coach charges the same rate. Period. Internal price competition destroys your brand.

### Mistake 7: Underpricing Enterprise Contracts

Corporate and franchise contracts should be priced at a premium to individual rates — not a discount. The administrative overhead, reporting requirements, and coordination effort are higher. If anything, your per-client rate for enterprise should be 10-20% above your standard rate before volume discounts.
